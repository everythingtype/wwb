Meet Jane.

Jane is the CEO of a financial institution that has always served a majority of women clients.
For institutions serving the low-income population, women often comprise a majority of their client base.

As Jane’s institution grows and changes, two big questions loom.

Jane isn’t the only financial professional asking these questions.
Because many financial institutions serving the low-income population share these concerns, Women’s World Banking developed a set of financial and social performance indicators which will enable institutions to measure how well they are serving women, both as clients and staff, while determining whether serving women is good for business.

Introducing the Select Five

Let’s look at these indicators more closely.

The Select Five can help Jane answer her questions.
These five indicators are the starting point— the minimum that all industry stakeholders should use to track and improve gender performance.

They are also the first step toward adopting the full suite of indicators
found at: http://bit.ly/genderindicators.

Indicator 1.
Identify the percentage of new women borrowers.

Beyond the number of women you are serving today, you should know if you are maintaining your commitment to reach women as your institution grows.

Identify the percentage of new women borrowers.

Indicator 2.
Identify the average loan size per woman borrower.

In order to understand whether your institution is reaching low-income women, you should assess the income or asset levels of women clients. Average loan size is one proxy.

Identify the average loan size per woman borrower.

Indicator 3.
Identify the women borrower retention rate.

Loyalty is one way to understand whether your women clients are satisfied with the products and services your institution offers.

Identify the women borrower retention rate.

Indicator 4.
Identify women’s portfolio at risk of greater than 30 days.

You should assess and compare the repayment rates of all client segments as a way to understand how women contribute to your institution’s bottom line.

Identify women’s portfolio at risk of greater than 30 days.

Indicator 5.
Identify women staff retention rates.

Assessing whether your workplace policies and conditions meet the needs of women staff is critical to fostering loyalty and productivity.

Identify women staff retention rates.

The Select Five

Thanks to the Select Five, Jane now has the tools and insights to answer her questions.

Let’s see what she found.

By looking at her new clientele, Jane realized she had a significant number of women clients, but that number was not growing proportionally as her institution expanded.

As a next step, Jane analyzed the women’s market in her country to assess opportunities to improve outreach.

Jane found that her women clients had a smaller average loan size than male clients.

In order to understand whether this was a result of actually reaching poorer women, Jane conducted a study to assess the poverty levels of her clients.

Jane’s female clients exhibited a higher retention rate than male clients, and this represented significant cost savings for her institution.

Jane followed up with a satisfaction survey which found that women liked the institution but were dissatisfied with some of the product conditions, such as weekly repayment.

Women clients had lower PAR, contributing to the profitability of Jane’s institution.

Jane further analyzed the repayment rates of different types of loans by gender to get a more nuanced view of risk to her institution.

Jane’s institution had low retention rates for women staff, indicating that the institution’s policies may not be accommodating women’s needs.

Jane also found that women were less likely to be promoted, which may have contributed to the higher attrition.

Now Jane can say that serving women is a smart investment and she is working to ensure she is serving these women well.

Most importantly, she has the evidence to back it up.
